How I Think

The beliefs and
mental models
I actually use.

01 — belief

Compounding is real, and mostly invisible

Whether it's exercise, knowledge, or relationships — the gains from consistency are almost undetectable day-to-day and shocking year-over-year. This is why I distrust intensity bursts and trust slow, ugly, consistent effort.

02 — model

First principles over frameworks

Frameworks are other people's first principles, packaged for convenience. I use them as a starting point, but I want to know why the framework works — or doesn't — before I trust it with anything important.

03 — belief

Honesty is a skill, not just a value

Most people value honesty but are bad at it — with themselves especially. I try to practice the kind that's uncomfortable: saying what you actually think, not what fits the moment.

04 — obsession

Right now: How systems fail

Engineering, law, institutions — everything complex eventually fails. I'm obsessed with the patterns in *how* they fail. Usually it's not the catastrophic thing. It's the quiet accumulation of small, reasonable decisions that each made sense alone.

Projects Lab

What I'm building.

Late Night Jotter
In Progress

"I keep having ideas at 2am and losing them by morning."

Why I Made It
Every note app I tried required too much friction. By the time I unlocked my phone, opened the app, chose a notebook, I'd already lost the raw form of the idea. I wanted one tap to thought.
What Didn't Work
My first version tried to do too much — organization, tagging, AI summarization. It became exactly the bloated thing I was trying to escape. Had to strip it to the bone.
What I Learned
Constraints are features. The moment you add "just one more thing," you've lost the plot. The best tool for a job is usually the dumbest one that does it.
Wristband Alarm
Paused

"What if an alarm vibrated you awake instead of screaming at you?"

The Concept
A vibration-based alarm that wakes only you — not your roommate, not your partner. Hardware meets sleep science. Wanted to use gradual intensity increase instead of sudden vibration.
What Stalled It
Battery life. Getting the haptic pattern right without draining a wearable battery in 4 hours was the wall I haven't climbed yet. Also: existing smartwatches do 70% of this already.
What I Learned
Always check if the thing already exists before building it. Research the hardware constraints before falling in love with the idea.
Scale Alarm
Shipped (V1)

"I forget to weigh myself but I'm tracking fitness progress."

What It Does
Integrates with a smart scale via API and sends a morning reminder only on days you haven't logged. Simple. Solves one thing. Doesn't try to be a full health app.
What Didn't Work
The API authentication was a nightmare — the scale manufacturer's docs were outdated by 2 versions. Spent 3x longer than the actual feature development.
What I Learned
Third-party APIs are a liability you take on. Document everything from day one. What feels like "just plumbing" often becomes the whole project.

Rabbit Holes I've Gone Down

Topics I've explored
probably too deeply.

AI & Alignment
depth: deep
Started because I wanted to understand what I was building with. Ended up reading alignment papers at 2am and thinking too hard about mesa-optimization. What started as "how does GPT work" became "can we even define what we want AI to do."
  • Is alignment actually solvable, or just manageable?
  • What happens to identity when AI can simulate people convincingly?
  • Who decides what "aligned" even means?
Law & Systems
depth: medium
Started after a contract dispute I witnessed made no intuitive sense until I understood how contract law actually works. Spent weeks reading case law and realizing that the law is less a system of truth and more a system of precedent and argumentation.
  • How do you design a legal system that evolves without losing coherence?
  • Is there a "correct" interpretation of a law, or just defended ones?
Sleep Science
depth: medium
Went in because I was sleeping terribly. Came out with a genuine appreciation for how much cognitive function is just "did you sleep" disguised as talent. The research on sleep debt is either motivating or terrifying depending on the day.
  • Can you actually "bank" sleep or is that a myth?
  • What's the minimum effective dose of sleep for different task types?
Failures & Lessons

The stuff that didn't work.
Which is most of it.

Quit something too early
Left a project 80% done because I'd solved the interesting problem and the rest was just work. Shipped nothing. Learned that the last 20% is actually where all the value is, and it's supposed to feel boring.
Lesson: Finishing is a skill. Practice it.
Over-engineered a simple problem
Built a system with 4 layers of abstraction for something a spreadsheet would have handled. Spent 3 weeks on architecture that served no one. The best solution is usually the one you can explain in a sentence.
Lesson: Understand the problem before falling in love with the solution.
Mistook activity for progress
Had weeks where I was "busy" constantly but measurably moved nothing forward. Scheduled busyness. The illusion of momentum without the reality. Still fight this. Probably always will.
Lesson: Measure outcomes, not hours.
Learned to code alone for too long
Spent months reinventing things that existed, avoiding forums and documentation because asking felt like failure. It doesn't. It's the job. Pride was the tax I paid for slower growth.
Lesson: Asking well is a skill, not a weakness.
Didn't speak up in a group project
Saw the approach was wrong early. Said nothing. Watched it fail in the way I expected. "I told you so" in my own head is worth exactly nothing. Speaking up is the job.
Lesson: Saying the uncomfortable thing early is kindness.
Let consistency slip for "one week"
Missed a week of guitar. Then two. Then six. Habits don't die dramatically — they quietly don't show up one day, and then again, and then you realize you've become someone who used to play guitar.
Lesson: Never miss twice. The second miss is the habit breaking.
